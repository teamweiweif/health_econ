# Source and Literature-Gap Memo: ARPA 400% FPL Subsidy-Cliff Removal

## Verdict

`POLICY AND LITERATURE-GAP SUPPORT STRENGTHEN THE CURRENT LEAD; CONDITIONAL GO`

The ARPA 400% FPL idea remains the best current SIPP lead:

**Did removing the ACA Marketplace 400% FPL subsidy cliff reduce uninsurance near the threshold? Evidence from a SIPP person-month difference-in-discontinuities design, 2017-2023.**

The external-source review strengthens the topic, but the contribution must be stated carefully. The gap is not "nobody studies enhanced premium tax credits." The real gap is:

**Existing work documents the policy rule, models expiration effects, studies Marketplace demand or affordability, and studies earlier ACA subsidy thresholds. I did not find a directly overlapping national SIPP person-month quasi-experimental study of the ARPA removal of the 400% FPL cliff using a local pre/post threshold design with uninsured and coverage-source outcomes.**

That is the paper's strongest opening.

## Policy Rule Anchors

### CMS official ARPA fact sheet

Source:

https://www.cms.gov/newsroom/fact-sheets/american-rescue-plan-and-marketplace

Policy content relevant to this design:

- CMS describes ARPA Marketplace premium tax credit changes as starting in 2021.
- CMS states that before ARPA, households above 400% FPL were not eligible for premium tax credits.
- CMS states that ARPA capped benchmark-plan premium contributions at 8.5% of household income for relevant households.
- CMS explicitly frames this as addressing the "subsidy cliff" for people above 400% FPL.

Use in paper:

- Establishes the threshold logic.
- Establishes the post-2021 policy timing.
- Supports a local threshold-based research question.

Limit:

- It is a policy fact sheet, not an empirical estimate of coverage effects.

### IRS premium tax credit eligibility guidance

Source:

https://www.irs.gov/affordable-care-act/individuals-and-families/eligibility-for-the-premium-tax-credit

Policy content relevant to this design:

- IRS guidance states that ARPA temporarily eliminated the rule that otherwise disallowed premium tax credits above 400% FPL for 2021 and 2022.
- IRS describes the baseline rule outside the enhanced-credit years as household income at least 100% FPL and, for most years, no more than 400% FPL.

Use in paper:

- Confirms the statutory income-cap contrast.
- Supports treating the 400% FPL threshold as a real policy discontinuity, not a researcher-selected cutoff.

Limit:

- It is tax eligibility guidance and does not by itself verify SIPP income measurement.

## Current Policy Relevance

### KFF 2026 Marketplace update

Source:

https://www.kff.org/affordable-care-act/what-we-know-so-far-about-2026-aca-marketplace-enrollment-premiums-and-deductibles/

Key relevance:

- KFF reports that enhanced premium tax credits were established by ARPA, extended through 2025 by the IRA, and expired at the end of 2025.
- KFF emphasizes that households above 400% FPL are especially affected by the return of the cliff.
- KFF reports large premium-payment increases and sizable projected enrollment declines in 2026.
- KFF reports that 400-500% FPL enrollees were a small share of 2025 selections but a much larger share of the observed/projected decline.

Use in paper:

- Makes the question current rather than retrospective only.
- Supports an introduction framed around the 2026 return of the cliff.

Limit:

- It is not a SIPP microdata causal design.

### KFF quick insight on older middle-income enrollees

Source:

https://www.kff.org/quick-insights/a-steep-subsidy-cliff-looms-for-older-middle-income-enrollees-if-aca-enhanced-tax-credits-expire/

Key relevance:

- KFF highlights that the subsidy cliff returned when enhanced PTCs expired.
- KFF identifies above-400% FPL, older adults, and high-premium areas as especially exposed.

Use in paper:

- Supports heterogeneity tests by age and by local premium exposure.
- Helps motivate why the policy effect should plausibly be strongest where unsubsidized premiums are high.

Limit:

- The current SIPP evidence has not yet shown a strong older-adult gradient. That null/weak result must be reported honestly.

### Bipartisan Policy Center brief

Source:

https://bipartisanpolicy.org/issue-brief/enhanced-premium-tax-credits-who-benefits-how-much-and-what-happens-next/

Key relevance:

- BPC describes the 400% FPL cutoff as the subsidy cliff.
- BPC states that enhanced PTCs temporarily eliminated that cliff.
- BPC gives a high-salience example of a near-402% FPL older couple facing a large premium burden after expiration.

Use in paper:

- Provides a nonpartisan policy-report anchor for the middle-income cliff problem.
- Useful for the introduction and policy-significance section.

Limit:

- It is a policy brief, not causal evidence.

### RWJF Marketplace Pulse over-400 FPL analysis

Source:

https://www.rwjf.org/en/insights/our-research/2024/10/marketplace-pulse-whats-at-stake-for-enrollees-over-400-fpl-if-enhanced-ptc-expire.html

Key relevance:

- RWJF identifies people above 400% FPL, older adults, and high-premium areas as the groups with the largest dollar impacts.
- RWJF estimates substantial premium increases if enhanced PTCs expire.
- RWJF reports that a large number of above-400% FPL consumers obtained Marketplace coverage during the enhanced-credit period.

Use in paper:

- Supports the mechanism story: above-400 adults can be price-sensitive when the subsidy cliff is removed or reinstated.
- Supports testing high-premium-state or high-premium-area heterogeneity where feasible.

Limit:

- It is not a nationally representative microdata causal estimate.

### Urban Institute coverage-loss projections

Sources:

https://www.urban.org/research/publication/48-million-people-will-lose-coverage-2026-if-enhanced-premium-tax-credits

https://www.urban.org/research/publication/who-benefits-enhanced-premium-tax-credits-marketplace

Key relevance:

- Urban models coverage and affordability consequences of enhanced PTC expiration.
- Urban reports that enhanced PTCs lowered premiums across income groups and extended help above 400% FPL.
- Urban projects substantial coverage losses if enhanced PTCs expire.

Use in paper:

- Establishes that the policy community is actively modeling enhanced-PTC expiration.
- Helps frame the SIPP study as retrospective micro-evidence on whether the original cliff removal translated into actual insurance coverage changes.

Limit:

- Urban's work is model-based and policy-simulation oriented, not a SIPP difference-in-discontinuities estimate.

## Adjacent Academic Literature

### Prior ACA Marketplace subsidy-threshold evidence

Source:

https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272718302408

Study:

Gallagher, Gopalan, and Grinstein-Weiss, "The effect of health insurance on home payment delinquency: Evidence from ACA Marketplace subsidies," Journal of Public Economics, 2019.

Relevance:

- The study uses the ACA Marketplace subsidy eligibility threshold and reports a health-insurance coverage jump at that threshold.
- It shows that ACA subsidy thresholds can generate economically meaningful coverage and financial effects.

Use in paper:

- This is a key precedent showing that subsidy eligibility thresholds can be used for credible causal inference.
- It also means this SIPP paper should not claim to be the first ACA subsidy-threshold paper.

Limit:

- It studies earlier ACA subsidy eligibility, not the 2021 ARPA removal of the 400% FPL cliff.
- It is not a national SIPP person-month pre/post difference-in-discontinuities design around the removal of the cliff.

### Marketplace administrative targeting and demand papers

Source:

https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.13791

Study:

Drake, Meiselbach, and Polsky, "Efficient Subsidy Targeting in the Health Insurance Marketplaces," 2025 working paper.

Relevance:

- Uses Maryland Marketplace administrative enrollment data.
- Estimates Marketplace demand and simulates supplemental state subsidies.
- Directly addresses the policy problem of enhanced subsidy expiration.

Use in paper:

- Important adjacent evidence that the policy problem is live and that subsidy targeting is being studied with administrative Marketplace data.
- Helps position the SIPP paper as complementary, not duplicative.

Limit:

- State administrative Marketplace demand and simulation is different from a national SIPP coverage/uninsurance design.
- It is not centered on a local 400% FPL ARPA cliff-removal diff-in-disc.

### Consumer behavior and insurer response to expanded subsidies

Source:

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12554641/

Relevance:

- Examines expanded premium subsidies in the individual health insurance market.
- Relevant for market and plan-offering behavior around enhanced subsidies.

Use in paper:

- Can be cited as adjacent Marketplace-side evidence.

Limit:

- It does not replace a national person-month coverage study of uninsured outcomes near 400% FPL.

## What Existing Evidence Covers

The current source landscape is strongest in four areas:

1. Official rule documentation.
   - CMS and IRS establish the 400% FPL cliff and ARPA's temporary removal of it.

2. Policy simulation and descriptive monitoring.
   - KFF, Urban, RWJF, BPC, and related policy organizations document who is exposed to the cliff, how premiums change, and what enrollment/coverage losses are expected after expiration.

3. Marketplace administrative demand and targeting.
   - Recent work uses Marketplace administrative data to estimate demand and simulate subsidy allocation.

4. Earlier ACA subsidy-threshold papers.
   - Prior academic work shows that ACA subsidy eligibility thresholds can affect coverage and financial outcomes.

## What This SIPP Design Adds

The proposed SIPP paper adds a specific empirical combination:

1. A national federal threshold change.
   - The 400% FPL cutoff is a real statutory/policy threshold.
   - ARPA shifted treatment at that threshold by removing the cliff.

2. A pre/post local threshold estimand.
   - Difference-in-discontinuities compares above vs below 400% FPL before and after the ARPA change.
   - The primary estimand is not a generic pre/post comparison.

3. Person-month microdata.
   - SIPP supports monthly insurance states and person-month coverage outcomes.
   - This lets the study use uninsurance and any coverage as outcomes, not only Marketplace plan selection.

4. Source decomposition.
   - The data now include private-source variables sufficient to distinguish direct-purchase/Marketplace proxies from employer-related sources.
   - The mechanism evidence is strongest among lagged non-employer adults.

5. A policy-relevant retrospective test.
   - 2026 policy debates ask what happens when the cliff returns.
   - This study asks whether removing the cliff in 2021 actually reduced uninsurance near the threshold.

## Contribution Statement

Recommended contribution language:

> This paper provides national microdata evidence on whether removing the ACA's 400% FPL subsidy cliff translated into lower uninsurance near the threshold, complementing policy simulations and administrative Marketplace-demand analyses of enhanced premium tax credits.

Recommended design language:

> We use a SIPP person-month difference-in-discontinuities design around the 400% FPL threshold, comparing coverage outcomes just above and below the cliff before and after ARPA's enhanced premium tax credits removed the cliff.

Recommended mechanism language:

> Source-decomposition results suggest that the direct-market mechanism is clearest among adults not coming from employer-related coverage, while full-sample source movements are mixed.

## What Not To Claim

Do not claim:

- This is the first paper on enhanced premium tax credits.
- This is the first paper on ACA Marketplace subsidy thresholds.
- This estimates the actual 2026 expiration effect.
- This is a clean static RDD.
- This shows a clean full-sample Marketplace enrollment increase.
- Older adults drive the SIPP result.
- Monthly SIPP FPL perfectly measures statutory annual modified adjusted gross income.

Finding:

- I found official policy sources, current policy briefs, simulations, administrative Marketplace demand work, and earlier ACA subsidy-threshold evidence.
- I did not find a directly duplicative national SIPP person-month difference-in-discontinuities study of ARPA's removal of the 400% FPL cliff with uninsured and source-decomposition outcomes.

This is not proof that no such paper exists. It is enough to support a targeted literature review and a careful novelty claim at this stage.

## Paper Positioning

Best title direction:

**Did Removing the ACA Subsidy Cliff Reduce Uninsurance? SIPP Evidence from ARPA's 400% FPL Threshold Change**

Best abstract framing:

- Start with the 2026 return of the cliff.
- Explain that policy simulations predict large affordability and coverage losses.
- State that less is known about whether the original 2021 cliff removal reduced uninsurance in national person-month survey microdata.
- Present the SIPP diff-in-disc design.
- Report the main uninsured estimate and the lagged non-employer mechanism evidence.
- Be explicit about placebo, annual-income, and source-mixing limitations.

Best publication identity:

- Health economics applied micro paper.
- Coverage-affordability paper.
- Not a pure Marketplace enrollment paper.
- Not a causal-ML paper.
